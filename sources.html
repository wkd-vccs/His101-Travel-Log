Annotated Bibliography: Roman Public Spaces and Structures (27 BCE - 180 CE)

This collection of primary and secondary sources provides the historical foundation for our journey through Imperial Rome. Each source has been selected for its contribution to understanding how Romans experienced and used the public spaces that defined their civilization during the height of the Empire.

Research Focus:

Cultural and social history perspective emphasizing daily life, political participation, and cultural identity in Roman public spaces during the Early to High Empire period.

Primary Sources

Ovid. Ars Amatoria and Fasti. Translated by J.H. Mozley and G.P. Goold. Loeb Classical Library. Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press, 1979.

Digital Access:
  • Perseus Digital Library
  • LacusCurtius

Ovid's poetry provides intimate glimpses into how ordinary Romans experienced public spaces in their daily social lives during the Augustan period (c. 1 BCE - 8 CE). The Ars Amatoria describes the Circus Maximus and theater venues as places for romantic encounters, offering unique insights into the social functions of these spaces beyond their official purposes. The Fasti details religious festivals and ceremonies at various temples throughout the Roman calendar year, providing essential context for understanding how sacred spaces functioned in Roman civic life.

Pliny the Elder. Natural History. 10 vols. Translated by H. Rackham, W.H.S. Jones, and D.E. Eichholz. Loeb Classical Library. Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press, 1938-1963.

Digital Access:
  • Perseus Digital Library
  • LacusCurtius

Pliny's encyclopedic work (77-79 CE) contains crucial contemporary observations about Roman architecture, religious practices, and daily life during the Early Empire. Books 34-36 describe the materials, construction, and cultural significance of major Roman monuments, including temples and public buildings. His accounts of religious ceremonies, military customs, and public spectacles provide essential context for understanding how Romans experienced these spaces during the Flavian period.

Pliny the Younger. Letters. 2 vols. Translated by Betty Radice. Loeb Classical Library. Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press, 1969.

Digital Access:
  • Perseus Digital Library
  • LacusCurtius

The younger Pliny's correspondence (c. 97-113 CE) offers personal perspectives on Roman social life and public events during the height of the Empire under Trajan. His letters describe attendance at gladiatorial games, religious festivals, and public ceremonies, providing insight into how educated Romans viewed and participated in public life. The letters illuminate the intersection of public space, social status, and imperial culture.

Suetonius. Lives of the Twelve Caesars. 2 vols. Translated by J.C. Rolfe. Loeb Classical Library. Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press, 1998.

Digital Access:
  • Perseus Digital Library
  • LacusCurtius

Suetonius provides invaluable firsthand accounts (c. 120 CE) of how Roman emperors from Julius Caesar through Domitian used public spaces for political and social purposes. His biographies contain detailed descriptions of gladiatorial games in the Colosseum, public ceremonies in the Forum, and religious festivals at various temples. Writing as a court insider with access to imperial archives, Suetonius offers perspectives on how emperors utilized monumental spaces to maintain power and popular support.

Tacitus. The Annals. 3 vols. Translated by John Jackson. Loeb Classical Library. Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press, 1937.

Digital Access:
  • Perseus Digital Library
  • LacusCurtius

Tacitus provides critical political and social commentary (c. 116 CE) on the use of Roman public spaces during the Julio-Claudian period. His descriptions of Senate meetings in the Forum, public trials, religious ceremonies, and military parades offer insight into how these spaces functioned in Roman political life. The work is particularly valuable for understanding the tension between republican traditions and imperial innovation in public space usage.

Secondary Sources

Favro, Diane. The Urban Image of Augustan Rome. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1996.

Digital Access:
  • Cambridge Core
  • Google Books Preview

Favro provides comprehensive analysis of how Augustus transformed Rome's urban landscape to create new forms of political and cultural meaning during the crucial transition from Republic to Empire. Her examination of the Forum Romanum, Forum of Augustus, and surrounding temple complexes demonstrates how public spaces were designed to communicate imperial ideology and reshape Roman identity.

Goldsworthy, Adrian. The Complete Roman Army. London: Thames & Hudson, 2000.

Digital Access:
  • Internet Archive
  • Thames & Hudson

Goldsworthy provides comprehensive analysis of Roman military organization, training, and daily life, including detailed examination of military camps and their role in Roman expansion and culture. The work examines how military camps functioned as temporary cities and how military culture influenced civilian Roman society during the Early and High Empire periods.

Hopkins, Keith, and Mary Beard. The Colosseum. London: Profile Books, 2005.

Digital Access:
  • Google Books Preview
  • Profile Books

Hopkins and Beard examine the Colosseum as both architectural marvel and social institution, focusing on its role in Roman society and politics during the Early and High Empire periods. The authors analyze how gladiatorial games functioned as tools of social control, political propaganda, and cultural expression. The work effectively combines archaeological evidence with literary sources to reconstruct the lived experience of attending games.

Humphrey, John H. Roman Circuses: Arenas for Chariot Racing. Berkeley: University of California Press, 1986.

Digital Access:
  • UC Press
  • Internet Archive

Humphrey's definitive study of Roman circuses, particularly the Circus Maximus, examines these venues as centers of popular culture and social interaction during the Roman Empire. The work analyzes the social composition of circus audiences, the cultural significance of chariot racing, and the political implications of public entertainment in Roman society.

Stamper, John W. The Architecture of Roman Temples: The Republic to the Middle Empire. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2005.

Digital Access:
  • Cambridge Core
  • Google Books Preview

Stamper examines Roman temple architecture and religious practices from the late Republic through the High Empire, focusing on how these sacred spaces functioned in Roman daily life and civic culture. The study is particularly useful for understanding how temples served multiple functions as religious centers, political meeting places, and symbols of community identity.

Research Methodology and Scope

Chronological Focus

Augustus through Marcus Aurelius (27 BCE - 180 CE)

Geographic Focus

Rome and the immediate environs

Methodological Approach

Cultural history and social analysis rather than architectural or engineering studies

Key Themes Addressed

  • Daily life and social interaction in Roman public spaces
  • Political and religious functions of monumental architecture
  • Cultural significance of public entertainment and ceremonies
  • Social hierarchies and their expression in public venues
  • Imperial ideology and its manifestation in urban planning
  • Military culture and its intersection with civilian life
